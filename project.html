Zara is a chain of department stores belonging to the Spanish group Inditex and owns brands such as Massimo Dutti, Pull & Bear, Oysho, Uterqüe, Stradivarius and Bershka. The group is headquartered in La Coruña, Galicia, Spain, where the first Zara store opened in 1975. It is claimed that Zara only needs two weeks to develop new products and get them into the warehouse, compared to the six months on average in the industry, and launches around 10,000 new designs each year. Perhaps the most unusual strategy in its policy is its no-advertisement policy; instead, the company prefers to invest a percentage of its revenues in opening new stores instead

Zara's founder, Amancio Ortega, opened the first Zara store in 1975. The first Zara store was located on a street in the center of La Coruña. When the Zara format became successful, the company began to expand and open more stores. Its first store featured low-priced lookalike products of popular, higher-end clothing fashions. The store proved successful, and Ortega opened more Zara stores in Spain. By the early 1980s, Ortega began to formulate a new type of design and distribution model. The clothing industry followed design and production processes that required long lead times, often up to six months, between the initial design of a piece and its delivery to retailers. This model limited effectiveness and limited manufacturers and distributors to only two or three collections each year. Anticipating consumer tastes ahead of time presented inherent difficulties, and manufacturers and distributors faced the constant risk of becoming overburdened with unsold inventory. Ortega sought a way to eliminate this model by creating what he called "instant fashions" that allowed him to respond quickly to changes in consumer tastes and emerging trends. Zara is a vertically integrated retailer. It designs, produces, and distributes itself. Unlike other clothing retailers in the same market, Zara controls most steps in the supply chain. 50% of the products Zara manufactures and sells are in Spain, 26% in the rest of Europe, and 24% in Asian, African, and other countries. While competitors outsource production to Asia, Zara makes most of its fashion items—half of all merchandise—in dozens of company-owned factories in Spain and Portugal, particularly in Galicia and northern Portugal, where labor is cheaper than in most Western European countries. Longer-life, basic clothing such as T-shirts is sourced from suppliers at lower costs, mainly in Asia and Turkey
